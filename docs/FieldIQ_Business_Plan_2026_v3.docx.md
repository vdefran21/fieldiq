# **2\. Problem Statement**

## **The Volunteer Manager Burden**

In recreational and competitive youth sports, team management is almost universally performed by unpaid parent volunteers. A typical season manager for a youth soccer team (8–12 players) performs the following work manually, with little to no tool support:

* Coordinates practice and game scheduling across 15–25 families with conflicting calendars

* Negotiates game rescheduling with opposing team managers when conflicts arise

* Sources and books field time through parks & rec departments or club directors

* Sends game reminders, cancellation notices, and RSVP follow-ups — often multiple times per family

* Coordinates volunteers for snack duty, carpools, and tournament logistics

* Acts as the communication hub between coaches, parents, the club, and opposing teams

Existing platforms like TeamSnap ONE, SportsEngine, PlayMetrics, and LeagueApps provide a structured place to record this information — with varying degrees of AI assist for internal tasks. However, none perform cross-team work autonomously. The manager must still initiate every action involving a party outside their own team, chase every confirmation, and resolve every scheduling conflict manually. New entrant Otto Sport AI (Jan 2026, $16.5M) targets this space with a club-focused AI OS, but its Otto Pilot feature handles inbound parent Q\&A and admin tasks — not cross-team negotiation. FieldIQ’s founding differentiator remains uncontested.

| The Core Pain Point When a game needs to be rescheduled, the manager must: (1) check family availability manually, (2) contact the opposing team's manager via text/email, (3) negotiate a mutually available time, (4) confirm field availability, (5) notify both teams. This process can take days and 20+ messages. FieldIQ reduces this to a single AI-initiated workflow resolved in minutes. |

# **3\. Product & Solution**

## **Platform Overview**

FieldIQ is a mobile-first application with a lightweight web companion. The product is organized into two layers: a standard team management interface (roster, schedule, messaging) and an AI agent layer that actively performs coordination work on behalf of the manager.

## **Core AI Agents**

**Scheduling Agent**

* Ingests player and parent availability via calendar sync (Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, Outlook)

* Detects conflicts across all parties automatically and proposes optimized game/practice windows

* Communicates with the opposing team's FieldIQ instance to negotiate mutual availability — entirely cross-platform

* Monitors weather APIs and proactively proposes reschedule options when conditions require cancellation

* Sends approval requests and executes once manager quorum is confirmed

**Field & Facility Agent**

* Maintains a live database of local field availability through parks & rec integrations and private facility APIs

* Proactively books fields based on confirmed schedule — no manager intervention required

* Monitors for facility cancellations and auto-reassigns to alternatives with manager notification

**Communication Agent**

* Drafts and sends RSVPs, reminders, and cancellation notices in plain language tuned to the audience

* Follows up automatically with non-responding parents after configurable intervals

* Multilingual support (Spanish, Portuguese, etc.) — a significantly underserved need in youth soccer

* Differentiates communication style by recipient: coach vs. parent vs. club director

**Roster & Volunteer Agent**

* Tracks snack assignments, carpool coordination, and volunteer slots with fair rotation logic

* Sends reminders and fills unfilled volunteer slots through automated outreach

**Game Day Agent**

* Morning-of reminders with field address, parking notes, and uniform color

* Real-time attendance confirmation tracking — surfaces no-shows to coach automatically

* Post-game recap and availability check-in trigger for next event

## **LLM / AI Cost Optimization Strategies**

* Use Claude Haiku or GPT-4o-mini for routine tasks (reminders, RSVP follow-ups, standard notifications) — 10–20x cheaper than full models

* Reserve Claude Sonnet/Opus for complex scheduling negotiation and conflict resolution

* Cache common agent responses (e.g., standard cancellation messages, field booking confirmations)

* Batch non-urgent agent tasks (end-of-day reminder generation vs. real-time execution)

* Implement per-team token budgets to prevent runaway costs from high-activity teams

* Build a hybrid rule-based \+ AI system: use deterministic logic for simple scheduling, LLM only for ambiguous conflict resolution
